    Kafka and ZooKeeper Operations Standard¶

    Purpose¶

    This standard defines how to run Kafka safely in production — including legacy ZooKeeper-backed clusters still present in the field — and how to migrate or upgrade a cluster without losing acknowledged records or unexpectedly replaying consumer work.

    The core judgment of this standard:

    Kafka durability is not “replication factor 3.” It is the combination of replication factor, min.insync.replicas, producer acknowledgements, leader-election policy, and the write boundary used during change.

    When to Use¶

    Use this standard when you:

    • Operate a self-managed Kafka cluster in KRaft or legacy ZooKeeper mode.
    • Need durability, monitoring, and migration practices you can defend in an incident review.
    • Plan a broker addition, rolling upgrade, metadata-mode migration, or cross-cluster migration.

    Non-Goals¶

    This standard does not cover:

    • Stream-processing application design such as Kafka Streams, ksqlDB, or connectors.
    • Managed streaming-service internals.
    • A complete ZooKeeper-to-KRaft migration procedure.
    • Active-active conflict-resolution design.

    Version Boundary¶

    • Kafka 4.x uses KRaft metadata mode.
    • ZooKeeper-backed clusters require procedures for their exact Kafka version and supported upgrade path.
    • Do not assume a broker binary can be downgraded after metadata, feature levels, or storage formats have advanced.

    Data and Control Topology¶

    flowchart LR Producers[Producers] --> Source[(Source Kafka cluster)] Source --> SourceConsumers[Source consumers] Source --> Mirror[Directional replication flow] Mirror --> Target[(Target Kafka cluster)] Target --> TargetConsumers[Target consumers] Metadata[Metadata mode: KRaft or ZooKeeper] --> Source Metadata --> Target Catalog[Topic, owner, ACL, and criticality catalog] --> Source Catalog --> Target Observe[Quorum, URP, disk, replication lag, consumer lag] --> Source Observe --> Target Observe --> Mirror Change[Change controller] --> Producers Change --> SourceConsumers Change --> TargetConsumers

    The topology separates the data plane from the control plane. Producers, brokers, replication, and consumers move records; catalog, identity, observability, and change control decide whether that movement is safe.

    Cross-Cluster Cutover Flow¶

    flowchart TD A[Inventory topics, partitions, ACLs, groups, and offsets] --> B[Start approved directional replication] B --> C[Validate naming, records, lag, and offset sync] C --> D{Target matches the declared contract?} D -- No --> E[Stop and correct topology or replay policy] E --> C D -- Yes --> F[Fence source writes at a recorded position] F --> G[Wait until target reaches final source position] G --> H[Translate and verify consumer offsets] H --> I[Move producers and consumers in one controlled window] I --> J[Confirm only the target accepts new writes] J --> K[Observe and retain source until rollback window closes]

    Product Integration¶

    Product capability Managed object Required behavior
    Asset inventory Cluster, broker, metadata mode, topic, partition, owner, criticality Make topology and failure domains visible before change.
    Topic and schema governance Topic config, retention, partition count, naming, compatibility Prevent unmanaged topology drift and unsafe destination naming.
    Identity and access SASL identity, ACL, TLS trust, replication credential Separate producer, consumer, operator, and replication permissions.
    Observability Offline partitions, URP, quorum, disk, replication lag, consumer lag Prove cluster health and cutover readiness.
    Change management Final source position, producer fence, offset map, rollback window Coordinate traffic movement as one bounded operation.
    Evidence store Topic inventory, offsets, lag graph, validation, reviewer Preserve the proof for cutover and failback decisions.

    Planning Checklist¶

    Area Questions
    Brokers How many brokers, on what storage class, across how many failure domains?
    Metadata KRaft or legacy ZooKeeper? What exact source and target versions are supported?
    Topics Partition count, replication factor, retention, cleanup policy, and destination naming per topic?
    Durability Do producer acks, min.insync.replicas, replication factor, and leader-election policy agree?
    Consumers Which groups move, how are offsets translated, and what duplicate or replay behavior is acceptable?
    Write boundary How are source producers fenced, and what is the final accepted source position?
    Failback Is there reverse replication, an intentional dual-write contract, or a reconciliation procedure?
    Security Are SASL, ACLs, TLS, and replication credentials controlled?
    Observability Are offline partitions, under-replicated partitions, quorum health, replication lag, consumer lag, and disk usage collected?

    Recommended Standard¶

    Durability is a combined contract¶

    For a topic that must not lose acknowledged writes:

    • Replication factor at least 3 where the platform provides independent failure domains.
    • min.insync.replicas = 2 for the intended one-broker-failure tolerance.
    • Producer acks=all.
    • Idempotent production when supported by the client and workload contract.

    Replication factor 3 with acks=1 can still lose acknowledged data if the leader fails before replicas catch up. The cost of stronger durability is latency and rejected writes when ISR drops below the required minimum. Consumers may still read available data, so describing the entire topic as “read-only” is imprecise.

    Deployment¶

    1. Keep broker or node identities stable.
    2. Store log segments on persistent storage sized for ingress, retention, replication, and recovery headroom.
    3. Spread brokers and KRaft controllers or ZooKeeper quorum members across failure domains; use an odd quorum count.
    4. Set retention deliberately per topic; unbounded retention eventually becomes a disk outage.

    Monitoring that matters¶

    • Offline partitions and under-replicated partitions.
    • KRaft or ZooKeeper quorum health.
    • Consumer lag per group.
    • Cross-cluster replication lag and checkpoint latency during migration.
    • Broker disk usage, JVM heap, and GC.

    Migration / Upgrade¶

    Treat the change as a controlled operation with a defined write boundary, acceptance criteria, and rollback owner.

    Rolling broker upgrade¶

    1. Confirm the official source-to-target compatibility path.
    2. Freeze incompatible configuration changes.
    3. Upgrade one broker at a time.
    4. Wait for the broker to rejoin and under-replicated partitions to return to zero before proceeding.
    5. Delay irreversible metadata or feature-level changes until the rollback boundary is understood.

    Cross-cluster migration¶

    MirrorMaker 2 replication flows are directional. Two-way replication requires two explicitly enabled flows, and topic naming, offset translation, duplicate handling, and loop prevention must be tested for the selected policy.

    1. Capture topics, partition counts, replication factors, configs, ACLs, consumer groups, and source offsets.
    2. Establish the approved replication flow and secure connectivity.
    3. Validate destination topic naming, records, partition mapping, configs, replication lag, and consumer-offset synchronization.
    4. Define the final source write position and fence writes to the migrated topics.
    5. Wait until the target has replicated through that final position.
    6. Pause consumers when required by the replay contract, translate and verify their target offsets, then move producers and consumers within one controlled change boundary.
    7. Confirm that only the intended cluster accepts new writes for the migrated topics.
    8. Keep source data and replication available until validation and the rollback window complete.

    There is no universal producer-first or consumer-first cutover order:

    • Starting target consumers first can drain mirrored backlog.
    • Starting target producers first can create a bounded target backlog.
    • Either can be valid when the source write fence and target consumer position are proven.
    • Neither is safe when both clusters accept uncontrolled writes and no merge contract exists.

    Validation¶

    kafka-topics.sh --bootstrap-server <broker>:9092 --describe --under-replicated-partitions
kafka-topics.sh --bootstrap-server <broker>:9092 --describe --topic example-topic
kafka-consumer-groups.sh --bootstrap-server <broker>:9092 --all-groups --describe

    Confirm:

    • Zero offline partitions and no unexplained under-replicated partitions.
    • Each critical topic has the intended replication factor and min.insync.replicas.
    • Producer acknowledgement and idempotence settings match the durability requirement.
    • Consumer lag is bounded and draining, not growing.
    • Cross-cluster replication reaches the agreed cutover position.
    • Target consumer offsets resolve to the intended records.
    • Only the intended cluster accepts writes after cutover.

    Known Failure Modes¶

    Failure mode Symptom Likely cause Correct response
    Acknowledged record lost Data disappears after broker failure Weak acks, ISR, or leader-election settings Align producer and broker durability settings; test broker-loss behavior.
    Writes rejected Producers receive insufficient-replica errors ISR dropped below min.insync.replicas Restore replica health; do not weaken durability reflexively.
    Consumers fall behind Growing lag Slow consumers, rebalance, partition skew, or broker pressure Investigate workload and broker capacity before adding partitions.
    Offset translation error Duplicate or skipped processing after migration Target offsets were not verified against records Stop cutover; map offsets to actual records and replay policy.
    Divergent writes Both clusters contain unique records Both clusters accepted writes without conflict handling Fence one side; preserve both histories; reconcile before routing.
    Disk fills Broker unavailable or cluster unstable Retention or capacity planning failed Restore capacity, then correct retention and alerting.
    Rolling restart causes risk URP remains above zero between brokers Next broker restarted too early Stop the rollout and restore ISR before continuing.
    Replication loop or topic collision Repeated records or unexpected remote-topic names Directional flows and naming policy conflict Correct flow topology and validate with isolated topics first.

    Rollback and Failback¶

    Before target writes begin, rollback normally means leaving producers on the source, stopping the migration flow cleanly, and proving that consumer state did not advance against target-only records.

    After target writes begin, safe failback requires one of these contracts:

    • verified reverse replication from target to source;
    • an intentional dual-write design with ordering, idempotency, and conflict resolution;
    • controlled export, replay, and reconciliation of target-only records.

    Without one of those contracts, routing producers back to the source creates missing records or divergent histories. Stop writes, preserve both clusters, reconcile records and consumer positions, and perform a second controlled cutover. Accepting a known gap is an explicit data-loss decision requiring business authorization, not a routine rollback step.

    For a failed broker upgrade, follow the supported recovery path for the active metadata and feature level. Do not assume reinstalling the previous binary is safe after an irreversible level change.

    Security Notes¶

    • Enable SASL authentication and ACL authorization; do not run an open broker.
    • Use TLS for client, inter-broker, and cross-cluster traffic where confidentiality or identity validation requires it.
    • Never publish real broker addresses, business-sensitive topic names, or credentials.
    • Restrict who can create or alter topics, ACLs, and replication flows.
    • Protect MirrorMaker and connector credentials through an approved secret-management path.

    Review Cycle¶

    • Owner: the platform/messaging team.
    • Re-confirm durability settings when a topic's loss tolerance changes.
    • Re-confirm version, metadata mode, and migration topology for every upgrade or move.
    • Re-test broker-loss, rolling-restart, offset translation, and failback behavior after major upgrades.

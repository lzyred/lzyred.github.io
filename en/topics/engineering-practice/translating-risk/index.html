    Translating Risk in Both Directions¶

    An engineer once stood up in a planning meeting and said, correctly, "our database is at 80% connection pool utilization and we have no headroom." It was true, it was important, and it was completely ignored. Two weeks later, a traffic spike exhausted the pool, the service went down for forty minutes during a sales event, and the post-incident review noted — accurately — that the engineer had "raised the concern." Everyone in the room had heard a fact about a connection pool. Nobody had heard "we are one busy Tuesday away from losing the checkout flow during the campaign we are all in this room to plan." The warning was not unclear. It was untranslated.

    This is the skill we mislabel. We call it "communication" and imagine it means explaining technical things clearly. But clarity was never the problem in that room — the engineer was perfectly clear. The problem was that a true technical statement is in the wrong language for the person who has to act on it, and the engineer's real job was to translate it. An engineer's core communication capability is not explaining clearly. It is the bidirectional translation of risk: from the language of technical fact into the language of consequence, and back. Most engineers do one direction. Many do neither. The rare ones who do both are the ones whose warnings get heard and whose deadlines get sane.

    The two languages¶

    There are two languages spoken around every engineering decision, and they do not share vocabulary.

    One is the language of technical fact: connection pools, p99 latency, replication lag, certificate expiry, cardinality, blast radius. It is precise, it is true, and it is inert to anyone who does not already live in it. A statement in this language carries no force to a product manager or an executive, not because they are unintelligent, but because the statement does not contain the thing they steer by.

    The other is the language of consequence: what happens if we do this or don't, who is affected, what it costs, when, and how likely. Revenue, customers lost, hours of outage, regulatory exposure, the campaign that fails. This is the language business decisions are actually made in. An engineer who can only speak the first language is, from the business's point of view, providing weather data in a language nobody on the bridge reads, and then being surprised the ship hit the rock they accurately described.

    Translation is not dumbing down. It is the genuinely hard cognitive work of taking "80% pool utilization, no headroom" and computing the consequence: under the planned campaign traffic, this fails, here is roughly when, here is what it takes down, here is what that costs. That computation is engineering judgment. Skipping it and stating the raw fact is not honesty; it is offloading the translation onto someone who cannot perform it.

    Upward translation: fact into consequence¶

    The first direction is the one the connection-pool engineer missed: upward and toward the business, every technical fact must arrive as a consequence or it does not arrive at all.

    The failure mode is specific and common. The engineer states the risk accurately, in technical terms, considers themselves to have "raised it," and is then genuinely aggrieved when it is deprioritized. From their side, they warned everyone and were ignored. From the business's side, they were handed a fact with no decision attached and reasonably moved on to risks that came with consequences. Both are telling the truth. The gap is the missing translation, and it is the engineer's to close, because the engineer is the only person in the room who can compute the consequence from the fact.

    The translated version of the same warning sounds entirely different: "If we run the campaign on the current database without adding connection headroom, I expect the checkout path to fail under peak load — probably within the first hour of the promotion — taking down the exact flow this campaign drives traffic to. Buying the headroom is two days of work. Not buying it risks the campaign's revenue." Same fact. Now it is a decision the business can make, with a cost on both sides, in the language it decides in. It might still get deprioritized — but now that is a real choice someone owns, not a warning that evaporated.

    Downward translation: anxiety into constraint¶

    The second direction is rarer, harder, and the one almost nobody names. It runs the other way: a pressure expressed in the language of consequence has to be translated back into the specific technical constraint it is allowed to move — and the one it is not.

    The scene: leadership is anxious. "We have to ship by the end of the quarter, the board is watching, this date is not negotiable." That is a real statement in the language of consequence, and it arrives as undifferentiated pressure on the whole engineering effort. The untranslated response is to absorb the pressure evenly — cut corners across the board, including the one corner that must not be cut. The deadline crushes the load test, or the rollback rehearsal, or the migration's backup step, because all of engineering felt the squeeze equally and the most boring safety task lost.

    The translation that should have happened runs backward: take "the date cannot move" and resolve it into which specific technical constraints can flex and which one cannot. "We can hit the date. To do it we cut the new dashboard, defer the refactor, and reduce the test matrix — those are safe to compress. But the database migration's rollback path is not on the table, because if it fails without one, the outage costs us more than the slipped date ever could." That sentence translates anxiety into a constraint, and it does something the raw pressure could not: it tells leadership exactly what their own deadline is buying and at what price, so they can decide whether the price is acceptable. Pressure that is never translated into specific constraints lands on the wrong one by default, because the boring, invisible safety task is always the cheapest thing to sacrifice and the most expensive thing to lose.

    Notice that the downward direction also protects the upward one. The engineer who can say "this constraint cannot move, here is the consequence if it does" is doing the same translation, pointed the other way — and an organization that has learned to hear that translation is an organization whose warnings get acted on.

    Why most engineers do only one direction, or neither¶

    The two translations require different temperaments, which is why people who do one rarely do both.

    Upward translation requires a willingness to leave the safety of pure technical truth and make a claim about consequence — a forecast, with a number, that could be wrong. Many strong engineers refuse this. They will state the fact, which is unfalsifiable and safe, but not the consequence, which exposes them to being wrong about the business. So they stay accurate and unheard, and mistake the first for having done their job.

    Downward translation requires the opposite move: the willingness to push back up against expressed pressure, to tell an anxious executive "no, not that constraint," which feels like insubordination and risks the relationship. The engineers who can do upward translation are often the most accommodating, which is exactly why they fold under downward pressure: the same desire to be useful that makes them translate facts into business value makes them absorb deadlines without defending the one line that matters. And the ones who can hold the line are often too blunt to translate their facts into consequence in the first place. Doing both means being persuasive toward the business and immovable on the one constraint that earns it. The rarity of that combination is why so many technically excellent engineers have no influence: the accommodating ones translate facts but fold on the constraint, the blunt ones hold the constraint but never translate the facts, and an organization only changes course for the engineer who does both. It is a learnable skill, not a temperament you were issued at birth.

    The translation test¶

    Before a decision meeting ends, write two sentences on the page.

    • Fact to consequence: "If we ignore <technical-fact>, <user-or-business-path> fails under <condition>, causing <impact>."
    • Pressure to constraint: "To hit <business-goal>, we can move <safe-scope>, but not <non-negotiable-constraint>, because moving it creates <failure-mode>."

    If the room cannot produce both sentences, risk has not been communicated; it has only been mentioned. The test is intentionally mechanical, because mechanical language is often what saves a decision from becoming theater. It forces the technical side to own the consequence and the business side to own exactly which constraint it is choosing to protect or relax.

    Counterexamples: not every fact needs translating¶

    Translation is for risk that crosses the boundary between the two languages. Inside one language, it is overhead.

    • Engineer-to-engineer, the language of technical fact is the shared language. Translating "80% pool utilization" into business consequence for a fellow engineer who can compute it themselves is condescending and slow. Speak fact to people who speak fact.
    • A purely technical decision with no business-visible consequence — which library, which internal naming convention — does not need a consequence translation, because there is no consequence at the business layer to translate to. Manufacturing one is theater.
    • When leadership is already fluent in the technical risk — a deeply technical founder, an SRE-literate director — the upward translation is redundant and can read as managing them. Translate the gap that exists, not the one the template says should.

    The skill is recognizing which statements cross the boundary between fact and consequence and translating exactly those, in the direction the listener needs. Over-translating inside a single language is its own failure of judgment.

    Practical Judgment¶

    • Before raising a technical risk upward, compute its consequence: what fails, when, for whom, at what cost, how likely. If you state the fact without the consequence, assume it will be ignored — and that the gap was yours, not theirs.
    • Treat "I raised it" as not yet having done the job. You raised it only when it arrived as a decision someone could own, in the language they decide in.
    • When pressure arrives as undifferentiated anxiety, translate it backward before you absorb it: which constraints can flex, and which single one cannot, and what the consequence is if it moves. Make leadership spend their deadline on purpose, not on the cheapest safety task by default.
    • Build both directions. If you only translate facts into consequence, you will be heard and then steamrolled. If you only hold the line, you will be right and unheard. Influence needs both.
    • Translate across the boundary, not within it. Speak fact to engineers, consequence to the business, and stop translating when the listener already shares your language.

    The engineer whose accurate warnings keep getting ignored, and the engineer whose deadlines keep crushing the one thing that mattered, usually believe they have a communication problem and a clarity problem. They have a translation problem, in two directions, and it is the most demanding communication skill in the building precisely because almost no one is doing both.

    Self-Assessment and Growth Path¶

    Most DevOps self-assessments ask you to score yourself across a handful of dimensions and add up the number. You will find a matrix like that below, because it is a useful starting snapshot. But the number it produces is the least reliable thing on this page, and believing it is the most common way engineers misjudge their own level.

    Here is the trap. A scoring grid rewards breadth — it goes up fastest when you are a little bit capable across many dimensions. But the thing that actually separates a mid-level engineer from a senior one is not breadth. It is whether you have gone deep enough in at least one dimension to know where its dangerous defaults are, and to refuse them under pressure. The score cannot see that, because refusal does not look like a higher number. It looks like the same number with judgment behind it.

    Why the score misleads¶

    Consider two engineers.

    The first scores a 2 in all six dimensions: total 12. They can deploy a cluster, run a pipeline, read a dashboard, configure a permission, maintain a host, package a template. Everything works on a normal day. By the grid, they are solidly mid-level.

    The second scores 1 in five dimensions and a 3 in one — say, change delivery: total 8. They are weaker almost everywhere. But in delivery they have shipped the bad change, watched it take down a live service, and learned in their body that "the pipeline succeeded" and "the service is healthy" are different claims. They now refuse to ship anything without a rehearsed rollback, and they can explain exactly why a green pipeline lied to them last time.

    The grid says the first engineer is 50% stronger. Production says the opposite. The second engineer has crossed something the first has not: they have hit the floor in one place, and that single experience reorganized how they treat every change. The first engineer has never hit a floor anywhere, which is precisely why they are dangerous on the day production finds one for them. Breadth without a single point of real depth is not a mid-level engineer; it is a junior engineer with a wider surface area of untested confidence.

    This is why the score is a snapshot of your inputs, not a measure of your level. The level lives in a question the grid never asks: where have you gone deep enough to say no?

    What "saying no" actually means¶

    Every dimension has a set of tempting, plausible defaults that are wrong under load, and capability is mostly the accumulated ability to recognize and refuse them. A few examples of the move, so it is concrete rather than a slogan:

    • In delivery: refusing "we'll add rollback later," because you have lived the incident where later never came and the first bad change became the outage.
    • In observability: refusing "we have monitoring, so we're covered," because you have watched a migrated alert go silent and a quiet system look identical to a healthy one.
    • In platform work: refusing "let's centralize this and teams will adopt it," because you have seen a platform become a ticket queue that made delivery slower while looking like progress.
    • In capacity and cost: refusing "it works in the demo," because you have met the node-level limit that let a workload schedule fine and then stall under real traffic.

    Notice what these have in common. None of them is a tool you learned. Each is a default you stopped trusting because reality charged you for it once. That is the texture of real capability, and it is invisible to a 0–4 grid. The grid measures what you can do; seniority is increasingly defined by what you have learned to refuse.

    Use the matrix, then look past it¶

    Score yourself — it is a fair first sketch of your breadth, and early in a career breadth is exactly right, for a reason that is easy to miss: you cannot yet tell which dimension your future will force you deep into. The engineer who stays narrow too early bets their whole level on one column, and then a role change, an incident, or a re-org moves the weight to a column they never touched. Broad-but-shallow is the correct hedge until you have enough exposure to know where to spend depth.

    Rate each dimension 0–4: 0 you cannot judge it; 1 you can follow documentation; 2 you can deliver routine work; 3 you can design solutions and handle exceptions; 4 you can define standards and improve them with data.

    Dimension 0-1 Points 2 Points 3-4 Points
    Linux / Network / Storage Only common commands Can troubleshoot common system issues Can design capacity, isolation, recovery, and security baselines
    Containers / Kubernetes / Cloud Can deploy examples Can maintain routine clusters and services Can design production HA, upgrade, and recovery strategies
    CI/CD / Artifacts / Environments Can run a pipeline Can maintain release flow Can design safe, reversible, auditable delivery systems
    Observability / Incident Handling Can read logs and dashboards Can diagnose common incidents Can design alert tiers, SLOs, and review loops
    Security / Compliance / Cost Knows basic concepts Can run scans and configure permissions Can embed governance into platform and delivery flow
    Platform / Standardization / Influence Serves only yourself Can package scripts and templates Can serve multiple teams and quantify platform value

    Now read the result the way it actually predicts production behavior, which is almost the opposite of summing it:

    • Your floor matters more than your total. A 0 in a dimension you are responsible for is a latent incident, regardless of how high the others are. Fix floors before you raise ceilings.
    • Your single highest dimension matters more than your average. One genuine 3 — one place you have gone deep enough to refuse bad defaults — is stronger evidence of seniority than six 2s. If your highest score is a 2, you do not yet have a place you have been tested; that, not your total, is what to work on.
    • The shape tells you the move. All-low: build breadth, follow the foundations. Flat all-2: stop spreading and drive one dimension to a real 3 by owning something end to end through a failure. Spiky with a 3 and some 1s: you have your first depth; now raise the floors that your depth depends on.

    The growth path is a sequence of shapes¶

    A career in this work is not a climb up a single ladder; it is a predictable change in the shape of your grid, and knowing the next shape is the whole of the growth path.

    You start flat and low — a little of everything, deep in nothing. The right move is not to raise the average evenly; it is to drive one dimension to a real 3 by owning something end to end, through a failure, until you have refused a bad default and been proven right. That first peak is the jump from junior to mid: you stop being someone who operates tools and become someone who has a place they can say no.

    Then the shape becomes spiky — one or two real peaks, several lows. The mid-to-senior move is counterintuitive: it is mostly about floors, not new peaks. Your depth in one dimension is only safe if the dimensions it leans on are not zeros — a brilliant delivery design fails on a foundation you cannot recover, and a strong platform collapses on observability you never built. Senior engineers spend years raising the floors under their peak so the peak can bear production weight.

    The architect and expert shapes come last and are different in kind: the grid stops being about you. The question becomes whether you can raise other people's floors and reproduce your peaks across teams — encode the refusals you earned into a path others follow without having to be burned first. The risk at this altitude is the inverse of the beginner's: leaving the frontline so far behind that you can only speak in principles, which is its own kind of floor falling to zero.

    So the path is: get broad, earn one peak, spend years on the floors beneath it, then learn to install your peaks and floors in others. At every stage the instrument is the same grid read the same way — floor first, highest peak second, sum never.

    The honest evidence¶

    When you want to know your real level, do not count your tools and do not trust your total. Ask what you have changed:

    • Did releases get safer because of a system you designed, or just faster?
    • Did recovery get quicker because of a path you built and rehearsed, or did you get lucky?
    • Did a class of incident get rarer because you fixed it structurally, or did it just not recur yet?
    • Did a team become less dependent on one person's memory, or did that person become you?

    Every "yes" with a mechanism behind it is worth more than any point on the grid. The point of the matrix is to find where you have none of those yes-answers, and go earn one.

    Once you know your band, use the Content Maturity Map to find what to read next, and the DevOps Capability Topology to see how the dimension you are deepening connects to the ones around it.
